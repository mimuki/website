Introduction

Tone tags are intended to clarify your phrasing and help disambiguate online conversation. Tone is hard on the internet, and their goal is to explicitly state tone in a way that removes ambiguity. I don't use them, and I don't like them, so I'm critiquing them at length here.

Disclaimer

Don't be mean to someone because they use tone tags. Don't be mean to someone because they don't use tone tags. Not everyone will benefit from the same accessibility tool, and that's okay. For those they help, tone tags are awesome and vastly improve online encounters. I'm just not one of those people, and I want to talk about them from my perspective.

All tone tags used are me naming them, not marking the tone of the previous statement. Unless I'm quoting someone else, that applies to everything on this website.

What are tone tags?

While increasingly common, there's a good chance someone won't know why I care so much about what looks like random slash commands.

Tone tags are short strings used in casual writing to inform the reader of the writer's intent, or intended tone. They've been around the internet for decades, but had a large popularity spike from reddit, where things like /s for sarcasm, and /j for jokes became popular, along with some more reddit-exclusive ones like /uj, which I don't care to explain. I like not thinking about reddit when I can avoid it.

I haven't cared to dig deep into the "oldest usage", but here's use of </sarcasm> in 2004. I hope MrPoopypants had a quick recovery.

Their second peak in usage happened in the late 2010s, where a whole library of common tone tags suddenly entered the speech patterns of many. This group tends to be the more inclusive, accessibility minded youth of the 'net, with a very large neurodiverse population.

Many lists of tone tags have been compiled over the years, and used to varying extents. These are the ones I encounter regularly in my circles, (on top of the /s and /j I mentioned earlier) defined to give the reader a good picture of what intents are seen as needing disambiguation:

  • /gen: genuine
  • /lh: light-hearted
  • /npa: not passive-agressive
  • /nm: not mad
  • /hj: half-joke

You'll notice a general trend here, of tone tags "softening" one's speech. It's rare to see /m, but it's counterpart /nm is relatively common. The lack of things like face or body expressions, varying your pitch or intonation, etc, all cause written text to seem "blunt by default" when compared to spoken or signed conversation.

The good

Tone tags have plenty of aspirations. Many people like using them because clarifying what you mean when it could be potentially read worse is useful!

They're especially popular in neurodiverse spaces, as many folks in them struggle with reading social nuance, or communicating their own. In theory, they're useful, predictable shortcuts to avoid conflict or awkward situations.

The bad

That sounds awesome, and it's pretty clear how they became so popular so suddenly. But there are some flaws they have that I don't like.

The first one, which is more clear if this page is your first introduction to them: they aren't always intuitive, and there's a risk the other person doesn't know what they are. There's lots of room for misinterpretation even if you do know about tone tags. I've seen /r (an uncommon tone tag for "romantic") mistakenly used for /ref (a slightly less uncommon tone tag for "reference"), which drastically shifted how the other party interacted as a result.

There's no authority on tone tags, so they spring up from community usage, and have fuzzy overlap that complicates things. A good example of that is the coexistance of /gen (genuine) and /srs (serious), which can largely be used interchangeably. A /ij (inside joke) is also a /ref (reference), /lh is often just a mild /j, and so on. I don't think an authority on tone tags would make the situation any better, but I wanted to point this flaw out for anyone who hasn't seen it first hand.

Lesser known tone tags are often met with a "what's /lyr?" or similar question. This makes them generally redundant, like all UAUIS (Unknown Acronyms Used In Speech). If you need to explain what it means anyway, why not just...explain what it means, without the tone tag?

Another problem is that not everyone uses tone tags in the same way. Some people are extremely generous, and use them in the same way people use "lol" for anything from "funny" to "I feel weird not saying lol here, lol". This causes the actual meaning behind them to get pretty vague- what does it mean to be /lh? Despite tone tags being used primarily by and for neurodivergent people, learning which tone tag is used when can be a whole challenge in itself if you're neurodivergent, speaking from experience. Some people "get" them better than others, because neurodiversity is not a monolith.

Some people use tone tags for the sake of a joke, like an innocent statement being tagged /threat because it's funny. These are pretty clear to notice, but over time I've watched this non-serious use get more and more difficult to tell apart from genuine statements. Even now, not even /gen can be trusted at times.

A very obvious ambiguity in tone tags is in /hj. What does it mean for something to be a half-joke? There are many interpretations, such as:

  • "Part of this message is 100% serious, part is 100% joke"
  • "What I'm saying is serious, but I'm saying it in a jokey way
  • "This message is somewhat a joke, but I haven't written it well enough to be actually funny/a full joke"

Which isn't too bad, except when you factor in that /hj doesn't tell you which parts are serious and which parts are jokes. This compounds with the issue of people adding them in for flavour like "lol", to cause it to be... bad. There's so much social nuance for a tool designed to disambiguate social nuance, it just feels entirely unuseful when used like this.

Some people also like to use tone tags to try and "get away with being an asshole", and use tone tags as a defense of their behaviour. I have personal experience of people saying hateful things followed by a /j or /ref etc, so that when I complained, they had the cover of "its just a joke, I added a tone tag so you'd know" or "I didn't mean to sound rude, I have autism and that makes me sound mean". These people know full well what they're doing, and are being malicious about it, knowing they can get away with it long enough to cause problems. Over the years this has gotten less common, but it certainly hasn't stopped.

If you're wondering why I refuse to use them myself, and generally request that others don't use them with me? That's why. Tone tags are too singed in negativity and deceit for me, that I can't take someone's /npa as anything other than "I am being passive aggressive and want you to know it, I want you to feel unsafe in this space". Logically understanding someone has good intent doesn't soothe my emotional, under-medicated brain.

Its also a great litmus test for knowing who will respect my boundaries, even if they don't understand my reasons. If someone is determined to do the thing I asked them not to, they're only going to breach my trust in more directly harmful ways if I let them stick around.

Potential alternatives

Since I'm so unimpressed by them, I'm "supposed" to have a perfectly thought out solution that covers every edge case perfectly, apparently. Prescriptively describing how other people "should" write sucks, and I don't like it. But for people looking for an alternative, maybe this will give some ideas.

The simplest fix would be to get rid of them entirely, and normalize clarifying in full sentences what you mean. There's absolutely no way that can go wrong. (this is a joke, and I'm making it on purpose to demonstrate my point)

It might seem clunky to phrase things longform like that, and it does introduce its own accessibility concerns, but in many cases a simple "(I'm joking)" is totally fine.

Some good intermediary changes would be to spell out tone tags as full words, like /sarcasm. Many devices let you autoexpand a shortcut into a longer phrase, so for no extra effort an entire talking point of mine is conviniently solved.

Honestly, any word or part of speech is going to have its meaning shift over time. "Literally" isn't always literal, and "/gen" isn't always genuine. Trying to make a bulletproof system like that is doomed to always miss the mark.

Conclusion

Ultimately, my opinions won't sway how tone tags are used. But, my stance of "please don't use tone tags with me" is an uncommon one, and naturally many people ask me about it. So I decided to write my feelings anyway, since I know it's just saving future-me some time explaining them.

If you're reading this because I didn't want to explain my reasons, and linked you here instead? Thanks for your time. It's super cool and awesome of you that you respect peoples boundaries, and want to understand how to be a considerate friend.
