    Content warnings Informed consent is cool Involuntary exposure therapy is not cool

    Informed consent is cool

    Content warnings are often associated with distressing topics like war and capitalism, but I love them as a summary of the potentially-impactful topics within, the "things that might make you want to (not) read this" summary. I generally don't feel distressed when food itself is discussed, but it's still nice to be told "hey, we're gonna be talking about recipes now". Maybe I have an upset stomach and don't want to hear it. Maybe I just don't care about recipes that day. But I benefit from the warning regardless, just like someone who genuinely needs that heads up for their own emotional wellbeing.

    It gives everyone involved the ability to participate (or not!) on their terms. And I love that. It reminds me a bit of how ramps are great for people with strollers, luggage, skateboards, etcetera- hopefully not all at once- even though the intention is for us wheeled folks to get around easier.

    Also, some content warnings are convinient as hell. 5000 character posts with a "long" CW save you scrolling for a week if your client doesn't auto-collapse posts. 18+ pictures with a CW avoid awkward moments on your morning bus commute.

    Involuntary exposure therapy is not cool

    Some people that are anti-CW have the logic of "but that doesn't help you get over your aversion to the thing!" which is... bizarre. That person can work through their stuff (or not!) on their own damn terms, and trying to blanket-therapy anyone on your website just cannot work, at all. If someone wants to become more comfortable with a topic, they'll seek it out when they can. If someone doesn't, forcing them to see it will only reinforce the negative association. It's foolish to think that intentionally ommitting CWs is helpful. Even if your cause is really noble or whatever, it just disproportinately hurts the marginalized people you want to help.
