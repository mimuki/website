Introduction

Veiling encompasses all sorts of intentionally covering or modifying (parts of) your body. It can be for a variety of reasons, but I'm talking about my relationship with it as a religious & spiritual act, that like all things also carries over into my mundane life. Maybe it's not for you, or you just think the scarves are pretty and don't want to get into the metaphysical. Do what works for you- this is just my experience of what works for me.

What does veiling mean to you?

Whether you're a curious website wanderer, who has no idea what I'm on about, or you've been veiling for decades, this is an important question. Religion and spirituality is not a monolith, and two different people can do polar opposite actions for the same goal. A great example of that is modesty- rejecting the concept of modest dress and choosing to dress provocatively can be a liberating act, but so can the opposite.

For me, veiling affects my life in so many different ways, it's hard to give a "single reason" as to why I do it.

Connecting to your Gods

As someone raised in a household with a very low-key interest in religion, I've always struggled with the idea of "religion time" and "secular time"; you're either engaging in your faith, or living a "normal", non religious life, not thinking about religion in the slightest. I'm most fulfilled when my spirituality is intertwined in my daily life- engaging with the Gods and spirits around me is rewarding and I always feel glad I did it after. But finding a way to invite that into your natural routine is really hard. Maybe you can go from never praying to praying every day, doing weekly offerings, and always working towards spiritual growth. But I can't.

Despite having been not-an-Atheist for more than half my life, I am really bad at "doing" religion. If I don't consiously push myself, I will default to a secular approach to life, even though I don't like it. A big key to counteracting that default has been small, yet meaningful actions I can do consistently. Which ties perfectly into veiling.

I have the upper body strength to tie my own scarves each morning, and the executive function to usually manage to do that. It's a significant action for many reasons for me, but I love having an easy action to say "Hey, you're a big reason I'm where I am today. This gesture is both a thank you and a promise, that I'll continue to work on myself.".

Daily rituals need to be simple and low key. This and the tried and true "pouring out a cold one for the boys" (offering a glass of water) are the bread and butter of my practice as a disabled man with more pain than energy to do things. But without anything else, just veiling to remind myself "hey, I'm not an Atheist" is hollow.

Body autonomy, in this economy?

People love to dictate how you dress, in both overt and subtle ways. Even as a man, there are a lot of expectations on how I "should" look, and I found myself dressing to a heteronormative, white person's opinion of attractive. I want to control when and how I am percieved, and veiling fits great into that reclamation of autonomy. I feel more comfortable with something on my head, so I'm gonna wear it. It's more "socially acceptable" to ask someone to put their hood down, even if they would prefer to keep their hood up. I've yet to be asked to take off my turban, so it's a great way to assert that actually, my comfort is more important than your aesthetics.

Veiling also made me familiar with modesty, which I have a... complicated relationship with. Ultimately my goal is to dress on my own terms, which means that I will show lots of skin on hot days, because it's damn hot out. But in general I've found a lot of comfort in showing a bit less skin, like wearing 3/4 tights under my shorts, which tend to land mid-thigh, which I don't like aesthetically, and it's uncomfortable when I'm in my wheelchair. That also has the bonus of queering my outfit a little bit, something I value a lot.

I definitely think women & femme folks in general are able to have more insightful takes on modesty than I can, but that's my relationship to it, at least.

My approach to veiling

It's important to start with this: everyone's relationship with veiling is their own, and I am no better or worse than someone else.

With that out of the way... I veil every day, for the majority of the day, even when I'm just staying home with my partner. I always veil when in public, and the vast majority of the time when with friends. There are friends who I'm comfortable with seeing me uncovered, but even then, I'm only doing that on my terms, which usually means I don't. But should I be in the mood, I would.

My go to styles are the old reliable turban- I have a method that's secure and gentle on my arms. On warmer days I'll go for a loose, over-the-shoulder scarf, but I don't wear that outside the house. I've experimented with a variety of styles, and every so often I take notes from Wrapunzel's video tutorials, but in general I stick to what works best for me.
